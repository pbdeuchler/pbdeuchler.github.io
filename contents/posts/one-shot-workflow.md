I am a little bit disappointed with my industry right now. There seems to be have been a massive unmooring of steady thought over the past year or so, brought on by the full advent of LLMs and highly competent coding agents. Much of this is driven by emotions from purported AI driven layoffs and belt tightening[^0], a mourning for the zen and art of coding, and a general identity crisis around who a software engineer is and what they do. Much of it is also a lack of imagination and a devolution to the lowest common denominator now that there is no longer a clear status signal between those who carefully craft and those who shovel slop. I am sure to many who previously considered themselves fine artisans it is easy to give up and start flinging vibecoded LOC over the wall. But I cannot shake the fact that this wave just _does not feel like engineering_. Every time I see a video or post about some new agentic orchestration framework, or some high throughput Jira to Agent slop pipeline, I am struck with an intense sense of deja vu. This obsession with tooling and productivity optimization smacks of engineers arguing about and ricing their terminals and IDEs, constantly looking for the holy grail of Vim bundles that will truly unlock their inner greatness. But none of this actually gets work done, none of this builds usable products, and I refuse to believe that the core role of engineering software systems is dead only to be replaced by piping customer requests directly into a coding agent.

Our industry has (notoriously) long struggled with the concepts of rigor, systems discipline, and formal standards... often swinging violently between overdoing it and completely ignoring anything that might resemble exactness. Taking a step back one can easily frame the recent LLM driven chaos as this finally coming home to root and many reaping the seeds they sowed long ago. The obsession with things like frameworks, leetcode, and arcane bit twiddling[^1] and disregard for basic, repeatable, and structured _engineering processes_ has set many up for a stunning disappointment. No longer does it matter how well you can wrangle that array when querying from MySQL. What matters is how you structure your work, how quickly you can develop a pipeline of repeatable processes, and how _reliable and high quality_ you can make the extruded bits.

With the benefit of hindsight, this is really nothing new. I know many have believed that the reason they earned their keep was because they created beautiful experiences: SaaS that was a joy to use, and APIs that inspired delight. But truly, what we as an industry largely do is allow companies to turn CapEx into OpEx while presenting a throat that customers may strangle when things go wrong. This is doubly, if not [triply true today](https://www.theinformation.com/newsletters/applied-ai/anthropic-jpmorgan-seem-agree-ai-eating-enterprise-apps-yet). Features are commodity, and to a certain extent they might have always been so. What now matters most is how well you can operate your service. Indeed I am increasingly of the opinion that as the next couple of years progress customers will begin to gravitate towards companies and services that are highly reliable and consistent to use, both due to what I forsee as instability in current offerings from poor LLM usage and because feature parity will be so easy to accomplish as the cost of LOC continues to plummet. In many ways the advent of LLMs might actually bring about the programmer nirvana: where those with the most simple, maintainable, and reliable systems win.

So why, then, are we seeing such a marked decline in code quality from users of LLMs, and how can we fix this? How can we build highly reliable services when we are not writing the code that runs them?

## Context Windows

At risk of outing myself as ignorant, I simply refuse to believe that people are submitting 25+ PRs a day[^2] of meaningful, maintainable, quality work. It is entirely possible that some might have codebases riddled with bugs, submitted by others, that they can then feed in an automated fashion to coding agents to generate this volume but that then begs more questions than it answers. I also refuse to believe that people are creating maintainable pieces of software by letting swarms of agents code for hours on end, generating tens of thousands of lines of code, and then reasonably grokking that body of work. True understanding is borne in repitition and action, the ability to read volumunous amounts of code and truly internalize that into a mental model is an ability that is extraordinarily rare and not at all sustainable or scalable. There is the argument that models will continue to get better, that we can accrue tech debt and rely on future iterations to bail us out. I doubt it. That does not mean I doubt models will get better, I simply doubt that context windows will get meaningfully large enough in the near future[^3]. Self-attention scales quadratically with sequence length due to the matrix multiplication cost of pairwise interactions between tokens. This makes GPU memory capacity and bandwidth _hard constraints_ on context windows[^4]. Indeed, despite the heralding of million token length windows my personal experience has been that regardless of model, context cliffs are a very real thing and have remained consistently within the first couple hundred thousand tokens. Even if this was not true, LLM context windows are still tiny compared to the human equivalent... we'd need to see an exponential increase in functional size for them to compete, and even then humans will have the advantage of _understanding more deeply as context grows_, as opposed to transformer models behaving in a unimodal fashion[^5]. I firmly believe that to build high quality, maintainable code bases with LLMs tasks must be broken up into relatively small, very precise, highly structured pieces of work and fed into empty or nearly empty contexts. This model utilizes the LLM at it's most effecient and enables humans to still maintain their superior model of how the broader system works as a whole.

Once you truly internalize how precious context is it becomes more and more apparent how important structuring and detailing out your prompt is. Much has been said about prompt engineering and I will not waste words here, but it still seems like most of the time when I see people struggling with LLMs my answer is still "prompt better". Any relevant files, relevant code locations, and relevant code structure should be explicitly detailed in the prompt, there is no reason to waste time, tokens, and context on unneccessary search or find tool calls. Even more advanced sub-agent usage with context compaction does not compare to a careful human enumeration of what is relevant to the task at hand in the initial prompt. For those that work in very large or dense codebases and are skeptical about copy and pasting lists of files every time they need to make a change, well, good news. We've created magical thinking machines [that can do this grunt work for you](https://github.com/karanlyons/claude-plugins/tree/main/plugins/claude-map). Additionally, unless you are building something entirely greenfield in a domain you know nothing about (and this should probably not be happening often if you are doing this as your day job) there is no reason for the LLM to be doing research or web searches when given a coding task. Any relevant information, best practices, or specific domain knowledge should either be in the prompt or moved to re-usable skills.

Remember there is nothing stopping you from using LLMs to generate a better prompt. Much of what I'm advocating for is simply to remove that work from the code generation task. If you are lazy like me it's often very productive to barf a couple sentences into a coding agent and have it do a bit of legwork, whether you do truly need some research, or just need to [clarify a plan of action from disjointed thoughts](https://github.com/ed3dai/ed3d-plugins/tree/main/plugins/ed3d-plan-and-execute/skills). The key thing here is that none of this goes into the actual code generation task, which should always be done from a sparkling clean session devoid of context or state, and that any prompts given to that code generation session have been fully vetted by a human to be explicit, simple, and well structured.

The better you understand the task you are asking the agent to do before hand, and the better you explain and structure this task, the higher quality result you will get. In my experience the best prompts resemble very small one pager design docs, are ~250-400 words[^6], and explicitly detail out data structures, data flow, and general code structures. In other words, the human still does the thinking, but treats the LLM as a highly competent code gen. I do not think this is absolutely neccessary in all circumstances, but I do believe this style of prompting generates the highest quality code quickly and effeciently.

# Prompt as Artifact

Better prompts also pay dividends with faster and higher quality code review. Code review has clearly become a major bottleneck as LLM code generation has accelerated at any organization. One of the major issues is not only the burden on other teammates to review a massively increased volume of code, but people submitting code that they themselves have not reviewed yet. By encouraging people to write more detailed prompts you create an environment where engineers are being forced to think at least somewhat about the changes they are making. Then by increasing the initial cognitive effort of thought and care investment in the outcome increases as well. I truly believe much of the current code review problem is that people are simply no longer thinking deeply about what they are trying to do. The temptation to slinging half thought through agentic output over the wall is a very compelling siren song, especially with the recent increased focus on raw output across the industry. It is also undoubtedly easier to read code when you understand what that code should look like in your head before you even view it. Understanding how the problem would ideally be solved and then pattern matching that against the code you see in the diff is the most effective and fastest way to internalize what is changing, how it is changing, and if it is correct and high quality. This further re-inforces the human's understanding of the system and how it is evolving, making further code review more effecient. A flywheel, if you will.

One can further increase the effectiveness of this flywheel by _checking in the prompt as part of the codebase_. There is no better PR description than the prompt used to create the code! This idea was widely discussed very early on during the advent of LLM aided coding but broadly dismissed, in part due to the rudimentary tooling we utilized then, but now that coding workflows have become more mature this idea is more in vogue. The shift to prompts as the human created artifact, as opposed to code is a very important one. Once you buy into this model you realize you can treat these prompts similar to how we treated code in The Time Before. You can ask others to review your prompt, you can use an agent to "lint" it for things like length and complexity, or scan it for reliability or security concerns. All of this before actual code generation occurs. You can include the prompt in subsequent LLM code reviews to improve the context around what has changed. You can re-run it through multiple pipelines, iterating on the original prompt and re-running until you get everything perfect. You can even begin to re-run old prompts sequentially through newer SOTA models to continue to refine a codebase.

# The One Shot Workflow

While I don't think it's useful to think of LLM coding agents as fancy compilers, I do think that analogy is far more accurate than "collaborative coding buddy". I think far too many engineers have begun to subtly anthropomorphize their agents and treat the chat TUI back and forth almost like productivity (it's not). Another trap seems to be the desperate need to be multiplexing agents across terminal sessions or bizarre muncipal politic cosplays, hopping between them constantly, and divising increasing more arcane ways of managing these multiplexed sessions. I'm very skeptical that somehow increasing context switching and tripling down on multitasking somehow creates _better_ results. But thinking about coding agents as compilers in a code generation pipeline solves almost all of these issues. We already have a very sophisticated and battle tested multitasking strategy for combining contributions into a larger codebase: distributed source control with CI/CD. Coding should be largely automated and unseen, curated by agent harnesses and well written skills. The end result should just be more pull requests that either get accepted or rejected. If rejected, iteration occurs. All of this has slowly combined to what I'm calling the _one shot workflow_. Which is a bit of a misnomer, becuase things are rarely a true one shot. But the idea is more that repeated, clean one shots based on changing prompts are far more effective than iterating within a stateful chat.

The workflow is quite simple, but it is remarkably productive and fits seamlessly into much of my existing tooling and setup.

1. Do research and preliminary work. As noted above, this often means working with Messr Claude to scratch together an initial stab at the work, doing research, and iterating.

2. Synthesize preliminary work and research into a prompt.

3. Add the prompt to a subdirectory in the project and push it to Github

4. Open a pull request, and fire off my one shot workflow[^9]

5. Review the new PR my workflow has created

6. Iterate on original prompt if neccessary, rerun workflow

7. Once I'm satisfied with the code, merge the code PR into the prompt branch, and then merge the prompt branch

This burns tokens, yes, but it also produces high quality results. Far better than being token thrifty just to end up with slop cannons.
